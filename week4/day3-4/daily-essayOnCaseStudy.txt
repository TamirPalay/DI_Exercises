RedAlertEDA: Real-Time Data Analysis in an Active Conflict Zone
During the recent war with Iran, I decided to upskill myself in the realm of data analysis. My project was to analyse the Home Front Command's Red Alert System: a large dataset where each entry is one event — either a Red Alert (RA), meaning an active siren; a Pre-Alert (PA), an automated warning sent when ballistic missiles are detected at range; or an All Clear (AC), the signal that the threat has passed and it's safe to leave the shelter.

The first task was to secure the data. This was done through an application to the Red Alert API, where I had to identify myself using my Israeli citizenship credentials. The API was then pulled through a Python script, and a second script was run to format the data for use. For example, every entry contains a list of locations — some returning in English, some in Hebrew, often combined as a single entry. The second script split and cleaned these columns to make visualisation cleaner and analysis more reliable. A separate CSV was also generated based on location alone, enabling geographic breakdowns in Power BI. From there, the pipeline produced three output files — raw alert data, an enriched main table with computed sequence fields, and a sequence-level metrics table — all linked in Power BI via a sequence_id relationship and refreshable with a single script re-run.

The analysis itself greatly helped me understand the situation on a practical, day-to-day level. I built metrics to calculate the average time between a PA and an RA for my area — knowledge that gave me valuable extra minutes to organise what I needed before heading to the shelter. I could also graph the frequency of specific attack types and origins, which allowed me to estimate quieter windows to get things done and anticipate when alerts were likely. It also helped me read the geopolitical situation in real time: on days with fewer alerts, it usually indicated that intense joint strikes from the US and Israeli armies were underway, suppressing launch capability on the other side.

The findings were striking. The average time between a PA and RA for my area was just over 6 minutes, and the average shelter duration between an RA and AC was around 14 minutes. Over 70% of RAs in the North had no preceding PA — almost certainly attacks from Lebanon, for which no pre-alert system exists. Perhaps most significantly, around 45% of PAs did not result in a following RA, which is a direct testament to the advanced air defence technology Israel deploys daily to protect her citizens. On timing, the most common attack windows were late afternoon and around 2am — when people are returning from work or sleeping — reflecting a clear Iranian strategic intent to maximise disruption to civilian life rather than purely military objectives.

Without this analysis, I would have been significantly more anxious: never knowing when I'd suddenly need to go to the shelter, how long I'd need to stay, or how much time I realistically had between a PA and an RA. Data analysis didn't just produce interesting findings — in this context, it produced calm.
